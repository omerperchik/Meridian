Governance

The governance structure is the moat. Meridian's authority derives from neutrality — and neutrality requires that no single actor can control its standards, scores, or editorial decisions.

Direct Answer Meridian is governed by a nine-seat board following the IETF / W3C multi-stakeholder model. Three seats are held by model providers, three by enterprise deployers, two by independent researchers, and one by a public interest representative. All board votes are public. The full scoring algorithm is open-source. The founding organization holds no more than one seat. Major standards changes require a 7/9 supermajority plus a 90-day public notice period.

Transparency provisions

  • Public votes. Every board vote — member, vote, rationale — published within 24 hours.
  • Open-source algorithms. Full ATP scoring implementation published on GitHub. Anyone can reproduce their score.
  • Independent audit. Annual scoring methodology and outcomes audit by an external academic institution.
  • Conflict of interest. Board members must recuse from any decision affecting entities they operate, invest in, or have material interest in. Recusals are public.

Anti-capture provisions

  • Founding organization limit. No more than one board seat.
  • Removal for inactivity. 7/9 vote removes any member with >50% missed votes in 12 months.
  • Removal for conflict. Sustained conflict-of-interest violations trigger removal.
  • Constituency replacement. Vacated seats must be filled from the same constituency.
  • Editorial independence. The editorial team reports to the Editor-in-Chief, who reports to the board as a whole — not to any individual member.

FAQ

Who funds Meridian?

Revenue comes from six streams: API access, premium reviews, enterprise licensing, certification maintenance, arena sponsorships, and training data licensing. No single funder provides a dominant share. Financial statements are published annually.

Can a model provider control the standards?

No. Model providers hold 3 of 9 seats. Major changes require 7/9 supermajority. Enterprise deployers, researchers, and the public interest seat form a majority bloc independent of model providers.

What stops commercial pressure from eroding neutrality?

Public votes, open-source algorithms, annual independent audits, and the rule that premium reviews buy access to deeper analysis — not better scores for equal performance.

How are researchers selected?

Researcher seats require unanimous vote of existing board members. Public interest seat is nominated by a panel of academic AI ethics centers, not the board itself.

Is Meridian trying to replace regulation?

No. Meridian is industry infrastructure. Its goal is to be the standard regulators build from, not against. The public interest seat and regulatory submission program position Meridian as an industry input into regulation.
